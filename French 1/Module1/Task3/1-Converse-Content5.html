Constructing sentences through structures - The verb sortir

The verb sortir means to go out, to leave or to exit and it is an irregular verb, which means that it doesn’t follow a conjugation pattern like other, or regular ‘ir’verbs.

Here is the present tense of sortir.

I go out/ I am going out/ I leave/ I am leaving

You (familiar) go out/ you are going out/ you leave/ you are leaving

He goes out/He is going out/ He leaves/ He is leaving

She goes out/ She is going out/ She leaves/ She is leaving

We go out/ We are going out/ We leave/ We are leaving

We go out/ We are going out/ We leave/ We are leaving

You go out/ You are going out/ You leave/ You are leaving (plural or singular formal)

They go out/ They are going out/ They leave/ They are leaving (masculine or at least one man and one woman)

They go out/ They are going out/ They leave/ They are leaving

For example:

  • Je sors avec mes amis ce soir.
  • Nous sortons tous ensemble aujourd'hui.

A complete sentence contains at least a subject, the person or thing doing, and a verb, what the person or thing is doing.

The verb finir

The verb finir means to finish/to complete and it is a regular verb, which means that it follows a conjugation pattern like other regular ‘ir’verbs.

Endings for –ir verbs



Here is the present tense of finir.

I finish/ I am finishing

You (familiar) finish/ you are finishing

He finishes/He is finishing

She finishes/ She is finishing

We finish/ We are finishing

We finish We are finishing

You finish/ You are finishing (plural or singular formal)

They finish/ They are finishing (masculine or at least one man and one woman)

They finish/ They are finishing

For example:

  • Je finis mes devoirs ce soir.
  • Nous finissons le travail aujourd'hui

Constructing sentences with Qu'est-ce que

The question: ‘Qu’est-ce que’ literally means – What is it that...?
However, it is generally translated as – What?

In order to construct a sentence with ‘Qu’est-ce que’, a subject and a verb are required.

For example:

  • Qu’est-ce que tu regardes? - What are you watching?
  • Qu’est-ce que vous mangez? - What are you eating?

Constructing sentences with de + le, la, les

The partitive article is used when expressing an indefinite quantity or part of something. (For example in English : Some or any)

The partitive article consists of de+ le, la, l’ or les. See below, how it is constructed.

de + le

de + la

de + l'

de l'

de + les

For example:

  1. De + le lait becomes Du lait – Some Milk
  2. De + La crème becomes De la crème - Some Cream.
  3. De + l’ eau becomes De l’eau - Some water.
  4. De + les pommes becomes Des pommes - Some apples

Constructing sentences with ce, cet cette and ces

Ce, cet, cette and ces are demonstrative adjectives. These adjectives are used to identify a specific person or thing. For example, in English we would say (this pencil, these chairs, those tables).

These four demonstrative adjectives must agree in number and gender with the noun it modifies.

For example:

Masculine Feminine

Singular

(this/that pen)

(this chair)

(this/that animal)

Cet is used before a masculine singular noun beginning with a vowel or a mute 'h'.

Plural

(these/those pens)

(these/those chairs)
