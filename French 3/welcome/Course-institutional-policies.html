Assignments and Final Exams

Assignments

Assignments are due on their due dates according to the Course Outline and the Course Overview and Schedule page in this course. Late assignments will be given a mark of zero unless otherwise discussed with your course instructor. The student must communicate with their instructor within 24 hours of the assignment due date. An extension may be granted if the student has a medical emergency or illness documented by a doctor's note. This note must be submitted to the student's registering college. If an extension is granted, the maximum period to submit the late assignment will be one week after the original due date. While you may discuss with others how to approach the assignments, the work you submit must be your own. There will be no make-up assignments. It is the student's responsibility to keep track of all returned assignment marks.

Final Exam

Please note: If you require an accommodation for exams and have an accommodation letter, please ensure that your test centre and facilitator are aware of this well in advance so they can make appropriate adjustments as required. Extensions for exams will be considered for students with documented accommodations on an as-needed basis and based on individual circumstances.

If your course has a final in-person exam, you are responsible for booking your exam time with your registering college. In most cases your registering college has a set exam schedule from which you can choose a suitable time. If you are a Durham College student you can register online

Final in-person exam: the exam MUST be written on or before the Saturday following the official end date of your course – no extensions are granted.

Final online exam: the exam MUST be completed per the due date posted within your course schedule – no extensions are granted.

If you have any questions about booking an exam time, please contact your registering college directly.

Academic Integrity

To understand proper citing, work through The Learning Portals module on how to cite.

Note that this course uses the APA style. You can find more information on APA referencing at the American Psychological Association and at Purdue University's Online Writing Lab.

You can also find your college-specific academic integrity policy on the OntarioLearn Portal.

Example of sourcing a website

In-line citation (within sentence that contains the source information):
Creating a blog is easy. It can be even be done within 20 minutes (Chow, 2020).

Reference (at end of assignment):
Chow, S. (2020, April 11). How to start a blog in 2020. The Blog Starter. https://www.theblogstarter.com/?msclkid=73b98a43c8c51d35d1ddd4ea210e3c8f

Example of sourcing a book

In-line citation (within sentence that contains the source information):
According to Lencioni (2006), the five inter-related dysfunctions of a team include absence of trust, the fear of conflict, lack of commitment, avoidance of accountability and finally, an inattention to results (pp. 188, 189).

Reference (at end of assignment):
Lencioni, P. (2006). The five dysfunctions of a team. John Wiley & Sons.

When in doubt, please reach out to your facilitator to ask for help before handing in the work!

Plagiarism

Your success as a student is important to us. Therefore, we want to ensure that you understand your responsibilities regarding academic integrity. One component of academic integrity is ensuring the work you submit demonstrates your own thinking and writing, while also referencing the ideas and writing of others appropriately.

Plagiarism

Plagiarism refers to practices such as copying another person's work which includes:

Examples of Plagiarism
Infraction Example

Paraphrasing incorrectly

substituting synonyms for individual words, but leaving the sentence in its original format; excessively using another person’s work, even if paraphrased and/or quoted correctly

Resubmitting work

submitting an assignment that you did in an earlier course (regardless of whether it was the same course or a different one)

Sharing your work

allowing another student to look at your work to get ideas for their own. If they plagiarize your work, both of you will be cited for academic misconduct.

Collaborating without permission

getting or giving help on assignments to be done individually; using someone else’s submission in whole or in part

Failing to acknowledge ideas

omitting in-text citations and quotations (if a direct quote); not citing the source of data, graphics, video or other content

The following resource provides information and videos to help you understand and avoid academic dishonesty:

The Learning Portal Citation Guide

Some courses provide marks for proper formatting of citations or other references. In those cases, even though you are provided marks for citing/referencing your work according to established standards, NOT citing/referencing your work is still considered plagiarism, as is the absence of quotations if you have taken an idea word-for-word. Plagiarism is an example of academic dishonesty and can have serious consequences (e.g. zero on an assignment, zero on a course, etc.)

You can also find your college-specific academic integrity policy on the OntarioLearn Portal.

When in doubt, please reach out to your facilitator to ask for help before handing in the work!
